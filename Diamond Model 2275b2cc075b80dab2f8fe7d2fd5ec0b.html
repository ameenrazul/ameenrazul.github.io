Diamond Model

💎 Diamond Model of Intrusion Analysis

The Diamond Model is a framework used by cybersecurity analysts to understand, track, and analyze cyber attacks.

It focuses on four key parts that are always present in an intrusion:

1. 👤 Adversary

The person or group behind the attack.

📌 Key points:

  • They have intent and goals (e.g., stealing data, causing disruption).
  • Can be individuals, organized crime groups, hacktivists, or nation-states.
  • Analysts study their behavior, tactics, and tools to identify patterns.

2. 🛠️ Capability

The tools, techniques, and skills the adversary uses to carry out the attack.

📌 Key points:

  • Includes malware, exploits, phishing kits, and custom scripts.
  • Can reveal the sophistication of the adversary.
  • Helps defenders understand how the attack was performed.

3. 🌐 Infrastructure

The resources used to deliver and control the attack.

📌 Key points:

  • Includes servers, domains, IP addresses, email accounts, and command & control (C2) channels.
  • Infrastructure connects the adversary and the victim.
  • Tracking infrastructure can help identify or block future attacks.

4. 🎯 Victim

The target of the attack.

📌 Key points:

  • Could be a person, organization, or system.
  • Understanding why they were targeted can reveal adversary motives.
  • Victim profiles can help predict future attack targets.

🧩 How It Works Together

Think of the Diamond Model as a connected square, where each corner represents one of the four elements:

  Adversary ↔ Capability
      ↓             ↑
Infrastructure ↔ Victim
  • Changes in one corner often affect the others.
  • For example, a new capability may require new infrastructure, or a change in victim may indicate a shift in adversary intent.

✅ Why It’s Useful

  • Helps analysts structure and visualize intrusions.
  • Aids in threat attribution (who did it).
  • Supports threat hunting, incident response, and sharing intelligence.
